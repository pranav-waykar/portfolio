Addressing the Design Frankenstein of Driver Data Module

Overview

Driver Data is an essential module of ZAIDYN Data Hub. It enables storage and collaboration of frequently updated data sets. We transformed this module by reimagining its design and functionalities. Faced with a client's request for new features, our UX team conducted thorough research, uncovering integration challenges. To address these issues, we opted for a complete redesign. Once implemented, we anticipate a remarkable 33% reduction in task time, elevating efficiency from 15 minutes to just 5 minutes.

Problem Statement

In response to a client request, the product management team aimed to enhance the Driver Data module with additional features. However, over the past three years, a series of well-intentioned feature additions resulted in what could be termed a 'Design Frankenstein'. This led to a complex and less user-friendly experience. The present challenge was to reimagine the module's design, harmonize all features—both existing and newly requested—without compromising usability, and elevate the overall user experience.

Discovery

We conducted user interviews to uncover user needs and goals. Additionally, we performed usability tests on long-term users to pinpoint improvement areas within the existing module.

Blending Insights From Various Sources for Seamless Integration

When we received the brief for this requirement, we were already immersed in the development of another feature – the 'Approval Manager' within ZAIDYN Data Hub. We recognized that this initiative would also influence the present project. As a result, we needed to integrate insights from three distinct sources into our design process: insights gleaned from usability tests and user research, specific feature requests from the client, and the potential impact stemming from the 'Approval Manager' feature.

Bringing it All Together

The next step in our process was to synthesize the information gathered. We identified that the Driver Data workflow will have two distinct pages. One will be the landing page where the user sees all the driver data objects. The second one will be the Objects page that opens up when the user clicks on any of the objects in the landing page. Next, we created 'See | Do' Maps with the objective of clearly depicting what the user would 'do' on each of the pages and what information they should 'see' in order to accomplish their tasks.

Design Decisions

We started with a focus on the Landing page, which serves as the hub for listing all driver data objects in both table view and card view formats. As previously mentioned, the usability tests we conducted with users uncovered several existing issues with the landing page. Our primary objective now was to rectify these concerns and concurrently introduce new features. This involved a comprehensive reimagination of both the page's layout and the design of individual cards.

We then shifted our focus to the objects page, which becomes accessible when users click on any of the Driver Data Objects listed on the Landing Page. For this page, a conscious decision was made to separate the read-only mode from the edit mode. This approach ensures the maintenance of distinct contextual environments, showcasing only relevant functionalities to users and thereby minimizing cognitive load. Additionally, we relocated information about dataset and mapping details to a different location, accompanied by a hyperlink to that dedicated page. This strategic move not only freed up valuable screen space but also prioritized critical elements such as the data table, bringing them to the forefront.

In the edit mode of this page, we ensured a clear visual differentiation between the table and its read-only counterpart. This strategic distinction aims to heighten user awareness and firmly establish their current mode. Moreover, we guaranteed that all CTAs on this page remain constantly accessible to users, regardless of their scroll position, accomplishing this without introducing unnecessary visual complexity.

Usability Tests and Validation

Usability tests conducted on the initial set of mock-ups aided us in uncovering several issues in the designs, particularly related to the functionality for column freezing. We successfully simplified this functionality and validated it once more. Concurrently, we held several Focus Group discussions with stakeholders to obtain early feedback and ensure alignment with our intended direction. Following multiple design iterations, we gained confidence in our progress. We subsequently refined the wireframes and engaged in sessions with the engineering team to assess the implementation feasibility of our designs.

Challenges in Implementation

Our workshops with the engineering team revealed that some of our proposals would be challenging to implement due to the extensive volume of data within a single driver data object. As a result, the team initiated work on several Proof-of-Concepts to evaluate implementation feasibility. Once the outcomes of these POCs became available, we made design adjustments that addressed the constraints highlighted by the engineering team, all while upholding the user experience.

Impact

Based on our findings from usability tests, once these designs are implemented, we anticipate a 33% decrease in task time, making the process more efficient by reducing it from 15 minutes to just 5 minutes.
